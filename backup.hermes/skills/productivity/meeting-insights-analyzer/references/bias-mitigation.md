     1|# Bias Mitigation Framework
     2|
     3|This file teaches you to recognize observer bias — the tendency to see what you expect to see. Read this BEFORE any analysis.
     4|
     5|## The Observer Bias Problem
     6|
     7|When you analyze your own meetings, your brain has a hypothesis about yourself:
     8|
     9|- "I avoid conflict" → Brain finds hedging language everywhere
    10|- "I'm a good listener" → Brain highlights questions you asked, ignores times you interrupted
    11|- "I dominate meetings" → Brain counts your speaking time but dismisses context (were you facilitating?)
    12|
    13|This is confirmation bias. Your observations become self-fulfilling prophecies.
    14|
    15|## Three-Check System: Before You Conclude
    16|
    17|Before reporting a pattern, run this mental checklist:
    18|
    19|### Check 1: Opposite Hypothesis
    20|
    21|State your finding: "I avoid conflict in meetings."
    22|
    23|Now imagine the opposite: "I face conflict directly."
    24|
    25|Scan your transcripts looking for examples that support the OPPOSITE hypothesis:
    26|
    27|- Moments where you named a problem directly
    28|- Times you didn't hedge or deflect
    29|- Situations where you raised tension intentionally
    30|
    31|Count these. If you find as many counterexamples as examples, the pattern is weaker than you thought.
    32|
    33|**Goal**: Find genuine counterexamples (not just silence = agreement). If you can't find any, you're probably hunting selectively.
    34|
    35|### Check 2: Context Variation
    36|
    37|Even strong patterns have context limits. State your finding narrowly:
    38|
    39|**Weak**: "I avoid conflict." **Stronger**: "I avoid direct feedback in one-on-one salary discussions, but I'm direct in peer planning meetings."
    40|
    41|For your pattern, ask:
    42|
    43|- Does it appear in ALL contexts, or specific ones?
    44|- Does the context explain the behavior better than personality?
    45|- What's different about the situations where it doesn't appear?
    46|
    47|**Example**: "In hierarchical 1:1s (power imbalance), I use hedging language. In peer meetings (equal power), I'm direct."
    48|
    49|This isn't a personality flaw; it's situational adaptation. Report the context.
    50|
    51|### Check 3: Confounding Variables
    52|
    53|List all alternative explanations:
    54|
    55|**Pattern claim**: "I interrupt frequently, which shows dominance."
    56|
    57|**Alternative explanations**:
    58|
    59|- You think out loud and interrupt yourself, not others
    60|- The meeting was fast-paced with overlapping speakers (medium effect, not personality)
    61|- You're the facilitator and naturally speak more (role effect)
    62|- This was one stressful meeting; in routine meetings you don't interrupt
    63|
    64|Count interruptions BY others of you. Count self-interruptions separately. This clarifies the pattern.
    65|
    66|## Confirmation Bias Signatures
    67|
    68|If you notice these thoughts, STOP and re-examine:
    69|
    70|1. **"This is obvious, I don't need to double-check"**
    71|   - Red flag: You've stopped thinking critically
    72|   - Action: Force yourself to find counterexamples; if you can't, you're selectively scanning
    73|
    74|2. **"The evidence clearly shows..."**
    75|   - Red flag: You're already at a conclusion; now hunting for support
    76|   - Action: State the opposite conclusion and scan for support equally hard
    77|
    78|3. **"All my examples fit the pattern perfectly"**
    79|   - Red flag: Real patterns have some messiness; too-clean patterns are artifacts
    80|   - Action: Look for the exceptions that should exist
    81|
    82|4. **"I remember this happening a lot"**
    83|   - Red flag: Memory is selective; you remember surprising instances, not statistical frequency
    84|   - Action: Count actual instances. Frequency will probably be lower than your impression
    85|
    86|5. **"Other people would agree with this pattern"**
    87|   - Red flag: You're anchoring on what you think others think
    88|   - Action: Ask them. Let their perception guide, not reinforce your hypothesis
    89|
    90|## Structural Checks
    91|
    92|### Sample Size Validation
    93|
    94|Before claiming a pattern:
    95|
    96|- [ ] Minimum 3 instances across 2+ meetings? (Or fewer meetings if very consistent behavior)
    97|- [ ] Instances separated by time? (Not all in one week when you were stressed)
    98|- [ ] Instances in different contexts? (Different people, meeting types, stress levels)
    99|
   100|If not, state clearly: "Observed in limited contexts; insufficient for strong pattern claim."
   101|
   102|### Frequency Sanity Check
   103|
   104|Calculate actual percentage:
   105|
   106|- You speak 60% of a 60-minute meeting = 36 minutes
   107|- Is 36 minutes "dominating"? Only if others average 5 minutes. If they average 12 minutes, you're speaking 3x as much, which might be normal for a facilitator
   108|
   109|The number alone means nothing. Compare to role and context.
   110|
   111|### Timeline Validation
   112|
   113|Don't mix signals from different periods:
   114|
   115|- **Mistake**: "You avoid conflict" based on meetings from both calm Q1 and crisis-mode August
   116|- **Better**: "In calm Q1, you facilitate collaboratively. In August crisis, you became directive."
   117|
   118|Context changed; behavior adapted. That's not a personality trait; that's situational responding.
   119|
   120|## Self-Compassion Check
   121|
   122|Analysis is hardest when you're criticizing yourself. If you notice:
   123|
   124|- Unusually harsh interpretation of your behavior
   125|- Willingness to excuse same behavior in others
   126|- Focusing only on flaws, not strengths
   127|- Setting impossible standards
   128|
   129|...pause. You're not being analytical; you're being self-punishing. Fair analysis acknowledges both strengths and areas for growth.
   130|
   131|## The Gold Standard: Collaboration
   132|
   133|The most reliable way to check bias: **get someone else's perspective**.
   134|
   135|Ask a colleague: "In our meetings, when I avoid giving you feedback directly, what do you notice? Do I hedge?"
   136|
   137|Their answer may surprise you. You might be hedging in your mind but coming across direct to them. Or vice versa.
   138|
   139|Use outside perspective to calibrate your self-perception, not to confirm it.
   140|
   141|## Applying These Checks in Practice
   142|
   143|Before you write your report:
   144|
   145|1. **State your pattern claim in plain language**
   146|2. **Run Opposite Hypothesis check** — find genuine counterexamples
   147|3. **Run Context Variation check** — narrow the claim to specific contexts
   148|4. **Run Confounding Variables check** — list 3-5 alternative explanations
   149|5. **Validate Sample Size** — do you have 3+ instances across 2+ contexts?
   150|6. **Sanity-check Frequency** — do the numbers actually support the strength of claim?
   151|7. **Calibrate with Outside Perspective** — if possible, check your interpretation against someone else's observation
   152|
   153|If you pass all 7 checks, you have a genuine insight. If you skip checks because "it's obvious," you've found a bias, not a pattern.
